About Us / Our Philosophy
"Breathe, breathe in the air. Don't be afraid to care."

Mission Statement

Air quality data should reflect reality—not an averaged, delayed, or convenient approximation of it.

Air quality is not an abstract number. It is something people breathe every second, something that quietly shapes health without immediate warning. In a region like Jammu and Kashmir, where monitoring infrastructure is limited and environmental awareness is still developing, people depend heavily on officially reported AQI values to understand what they are exposed to. That dependence demands accuracy, transparency, and accountability.

Breathe was started by a small group of Class 10 students as an attempt to understand local air quality beyond what was being reported. What began as a simple investigation has grown into a funded, community-supported initiative. By deploying independent sensors and comparing them against publicly available data, we have consistently observed gaps between reported AQI values and actual on-ground conditions.

Our mission is not just to provide data, but to restore trust in it—by building a system that is grounded in measurement, transparent in methodology, and open for anyone to verify, question, and improve.

Philosophy

Open source, for us, is not a feature—it is the foundation. The code is open so it can be audited. The methodology is documented so it can be challenged. The data is accessible so it can be used, questioned, and improved. Systems that affect public health should not operate behind opacity.

Current air quality reporting often fails this standard. A major issue lies in how pollution is represented through extended averages, such as 30-day smoothing. Pollution is not experienced over a month—it is experienced in spikes, on specific days and hours. When those spikes are averaged out, the severity of exposure is hidden. A dangerous day becomes statistically invisible, replaced by a misleading sense of safety.

There are also structural inconsistencies in monitoring itself. Sensors may go offline or fail to report, yet these gaps are rarely communicated clearly. In a region with already limited monitoring coverage, even temporary outages distort the overall picture. When these gaps are filled with modeled estimates without transparency, the data begins to lose its connection with what people are actually breathing.

Equally concerning is the lack of access to raw data. The public is presented with final AQI values, but not the underlying measurements. Without granular, real-time data, independent verification becomes impossible. Researchers cannot validate findings, and citizens cannot question the system that informs their daily decisions.

This project exists to counter that. Not through rhetoric, but through construction—by building a system that prioritizes ground truth, transparency, and verifiability. Our goal is to expose gaps where they exist, push for higher standards, and contribute to a system where environmental data reflects reality with precision and honesty.
