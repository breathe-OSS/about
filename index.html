Mission Statement

Information about the air people breathe every day should be freely accessible.

BreatheOSS was built on that conviction. We are an accurate, transparent and community-maintained AQI monitoring platform, and we are open in every direction that word can mean.

Air quality is not an abstract number. It is something people breathe every second, something that quietly affects health without immediate warning. In a region like Jammu and Kashmir, where environmental awareness is still developing and monitoring is limited, people depend heavily on officially reported AQI values to understand what they are exposed to. That dependence demands responsibility. The data must reflect reality, not a diluted or convenient version of it.

It is against this backdrop that Breathe was started by a small group of Class 10 students. What began as a simple attempt to understand local air quality has grown into a funded, community-supported initiative. By setting up our own sensors and comparing them with publicly available data, we have observed clear differences between reported AQI values and actual on-ground conditions.

Philosophy

Open source is not just a licensing decision for us. The code is open so anyone can audit what we claim. The methodology is documented so anyone can challenge it. The data is free so anyone can use it. Unfortunately, that is not always the case. A major issue lies in how air quality is presented, often through extended averages such as 30-day smoothing. Pollution is not experienced over a month; it is experienced in sudden spikes, on specific days and hours. When these spikes are averaged out, the seriousness of exposure is hidden. A highly polluted day gets buried under weeks of cleaner air, giving a misleading sense of safety. There are also troubling inconsistencies in how monitoring stations function. Sensors are sometimes inactive or not reporting for periods of time, yet this is rarely made clear to the public. In a region with already limited monitoring points, even temporary shutdowns can distort the overall picture. Equally concerning is the lack of accessible raw data. The public is shown final numbers, but not the underlying measurements. Without open access to real-time, detailed data, independent verification becomes impossible. This project exists to counter that. By building a transparent, verifiable system grounded in real measurements, we aim to push for better data practices, greater accountability, and a more informed public in Jammu and Kashmir.
